Project Scope

Literature Survey

Happiness is a growing research area in economics. According to Kahneman and Krueger (2006), economic papers analyzing data on self-reported life satisfaction or happiness increased from just four papers (1991–95) to over 100 papers (2001–05). Notable reviews include Layard's *Happiness – Lessons from a New Science* (2005), *Happiness in Economics* (2002) edited by Easterlin, and *Happiness and Economics* (2002) by Frey and Stutzer. Economists have examined happiness in connection with factors like earnings, economic growth, income inequality, and more. They propose that if economics aims to maximize human happiness, it should directly study it, though happiness is subjectively defined (Layard, 2005).

Notably, age has a minimal effect on happiness (Frey and Stutzer, 2002). However, Hirvonen and Mangeloja (2005) found that older Finns report lower happiness than younger Finns, which is an intriguing comparison given that over 95% of respondents in the current study are between 18 and 28 years old. Additionally, Blanchflower and Oswald (1997) showed that happiness among young people in the US and Europe increased from the 1970s to the 1990s, likely driven by education. Their data suggests that higher education (beyond age 18) significantly benefited Europeans under 30.

Research Gap

Studies by Diener et al. (1999) and Argyle (2002) show a modest but positive correlation between education and happiness, typically linked to better jobs, occupational status, and income. While education may indirectly increase happiness, this only occurs post-education, prompting questions about university students' satisfaction during their studies. Investigating factors that influence student satisfaction is valuable for educational policy planners aiming to improve learning outcomes and motivation among future professionals.

This study builds upon Chan, Miller, and Tcha (2005) and Mangeloja and Hirvonen's work on student happiness, focusing on university students in Australia and Finland, respectively.

Research Problem & Solution

This study explores factors contributing to student happiness, such as academic achievement, social connections, relationship involvement, and financial stability. By understanding these factors, the research seeks to inform institutional practices aimed at fostering happier, healthier, and more successful students in higher education.


Relationship Problems

We developed a social chat app using React, Zustand, Dialogflow, and Firebase to enhance student social interactions. The app offers peer-to-peer communication and a rating system for friendliness. Data is stored securely in Firebase Firestore, with user feedback collected to assess its impact on social engagement.


Academic Struggles

Our engagement detection system utilizes real-time video processing to monitor student participation in e-learning. Using Mediapipe’s face mesh model, we track facial landmarks to measure engagement. Metrics like the Eye Aspect Ratio (EAR) and head posture monitor attentiveness, with data stored in CSV files for analysis.


Social Isolation

A VRChat world was designed to address social isolation, incorporating a feedback system powered by machine learning for user sentiment analysis. This continuous feedback enhances the virtual environment, fostering a positive and engaging space.


Financial Burden

The Personal Expense Tracker assists users with financial management through automated tracking and forecasting. Using ARIMA for time series forecasting and OCR for document data extraction, this tool offers accurate financial predictions and streamlines expense management, enhancing financial stability.

Methodology


Relationship chat app

social chat app utilizes a modern stack to facilitate seamless communication and real-time feedback. React is used for front-end development, providing dynamic and responsive user interfaces with reusable components for an engaging user experience.


engagement Detection system

The social chat app utilizes a modern stack including React for a responsive user interface. The engagement detection module analyzes video feeds in real-time, detecting inattention through indicators like blinking and head orientation, then adjusting engagement scores accordingly.


ARIMA Methodology

The ARIMA model forecasts financial burdens based on historical data, with components including:

  • AutoRegressive (AR) Term: Influence of past values.
  • Integrated (I) Term: Differencing for stationarity.
  • Moving Average (MA) Term: Effect of past errors on current values.

OCR Methodology

OCR extracts data from financial documents through:

  • Image Preprocessing: Binarization and noise reduction.
  • Text Extraction: Using Tesseract OCR to convert images to text.
  • Data Integration: Parsed data is recorded in the system for automated expense tracking.

Virtual Environment

The VRChat environment leverages a feedback system driven by machine learning to continually improve based on user feedback, fostering a dynamic and supportive virtual community.
